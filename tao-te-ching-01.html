A Glimpse from the Bridge

In an ink-washed landscape of a rugged mountainside, a bridge crosses a riverbed with scattered stones. Notice a cave, that is half lit by the morning sun. This 17th-century painting by Chinese master Gong Xian is visually striking and together with his poem echo themes found in the Tao Te Ching, particularly the idea that a fleeting moment can reveal something timeless.

The Tao Te Ching opens with a distinction between the "named" and the "nameless." The "named" is the mother of the myriad things, the visible world we can label: a tree, a rock, a river. The "nameless," by contrast, is the source, the ungraspable unity behind all forms. Gong's painting seems to hold both. While our mind delineates individual objects, the broader forces that shaped the landscape point toward the nameless. Note how the rushing stream, which becomes the heart of the inscribedpoem, seems like a dry riverbed in the painting. Is this a deliberate non-depiction, or even an anti-depiction, to create a sense of longing or anticipation that the poem's conclusion fulfills?

The poem adds a voice to the landscape, setting a tone of stillness and cyclical change:

Inside the cave, that immortal dwelling, the gate of dawn opens,
Vermilion clouds disgorge the sun, only to swallow it again.

This passage connects to the Tao, both impersonal and, through the poet's eyes, personified. The cave, representing deep geological time, is lit by the light of a single morning, combining the unhurried with the swift. The clouds "disgorge" and "swallow" the sun. From the narrator's point of view, it seems like a game is being played between them. We know, of course, that this is a human interpretation of a vast, indifferent process. And yet, in a deeper sense, the sun and clouds are partners in the water cycle, a 'game' that has shaped the earth throughout its history.

Then Gong narrows this vast panorama into a single, human moment:

As I walk upon the bridge to dry my hair,

Here, the figure on the bridge becomes a lens for exploring the Taoist path. The bridge itself, a man-made structure shaped by intention, rests within a wild, untamed landscape. It offers passage, but is surrounded by forces beyond control. By lifting the narrator above the water, it provides a new perspective. The proposed act of drying one's hair in the sun and wind is simple, almost unremarkable, yet moving in its humanity. There is no striving, only a gentle use of nature through doing nothing special but pausing on a bridge; body, wind, water, and sun joining in a shared rhythm.

And then the absorbing last line:

I catch a glimpse of fleeting time collapsing in the rushing stream.

In a state of suspension, the narrator looks down. He doesn't see a vision or a deity. What he sees is simple, yet profound: water rushing beneath him, carrying with it something more than motion. In that moment, time reveals itself not as a steady flow, but as something collapsing inward, presenting not the force that ages and erodes, but the stillness of the present, where time seems to vanish altogether. This is the paradox at the heart of Gong Xian's vision. Time moves forward, yet dissolves when looked at from certain angles, in this case, a glimpse from the bridge.
